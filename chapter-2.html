Chapter 2. Mathematical Foundations

This chapter reviews the mathematical background needed throughout the book. The motivating example in Chapter 1 uses the Yoneda Lemma (Theorem 1.1) and the Gaussian integral ((1.1)).

Definition 2.1 (Category)

A category consists of objects, morphisms, composition, and identity satisfying the associativity and identity laws.

Limits and Colimits

A limit of a diagram \(D : \mathcal{J} \to \mathcal{C}\) is a universal cone over \(D\). See Definition 2.1 for the definition of a category.

Limits generalise products, pullbacks, and equalisers; see Section 2.1.

\[\lim_{\leftarrow} D \cong \int_{j \in \mathcal{J}} D(j)\](2.1)

The end formula (2.1) generalises the Gaussian integral from (1.1) in the sense that both express universal constructions.

Remark

Colimits are the dual notion, obtained by reversing all arrows.

Proposition 2.2 (Existence of Limits)

A category \(\mathcal{C}\) has all small limits if and only if it has all small products and equalisers.

  • Products are limits over the discrete diagram.

  • Equalisers are limits over the parallel-arrows diagram.

  • Pullbacks are limits over the cospan diagram.
